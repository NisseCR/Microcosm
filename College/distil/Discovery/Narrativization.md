## Music, Narrative & Imagination — Key Research

### 1. Music and Narrative Imagining (Core Topic)

The most directly relevant body of work comes from **Elizabeth Hellmuth Margulis** at Princeton. Her lab has spent a decade studying what she calls _narrativization_ — the tendency of listeners to imagine stories while hearing instrumental music.

Musical excerpts vary along several dimensions: _narrativity_ (the likelihood any individual will imagine a story at all), and _narrative engagement_ (the degree to which that story feels vivid and absorbing, coming to mind effortlessly with clear characters, events, and settings). [Music Theory Online](https://mtosmt.org/issues/mto.22.28.4/mto.22.28.4.margulis.html)

Critically for your project, using tools from natural language processing, Margulis et al. found that listeners provide highly similar stories to the same musical excerpts when they share an underlying culture — but when they don't share culture, the generated stories show limited overlap. Music is neither an abstract stimulus nor a universal language, but has semantic affordances shaped by culture. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35064081/)

Margulis & Jakubowski (2024) further argue that music's capacity to reliably cue both autobiographical memories and fictional imaginings can be leveraged to better understand the relationship and interdependence between memory and imagination more generally. Musical excerpts are extended in time and can influence the trajectory of a memory or imagining dynamically as it develops, allowing for precise manipulation of implied semantic space. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09637214231217229)

Key papers to read:

- _Narratives imagined in response to instrumental music reveal culture-bounded intersubjectivity_ — PNAS (2022)
- _Intersubjectivity and Shared Dynamic Structure in Narrative Imaginings to Music_ — Music Theory Online (2022)
- _When Did That Happen? The Dynamic Unfolding of Perceived Musical Narrative_ — Cognition (2022)
- _Music, Memory, and Imagination_ — Psychological Science (2024)

### 2. Music-Evoked Visual Mental Imagery & Mind-Wandering

This cluster of research links directly to how music — especially ambient and emotionally evocative music — triggers imaginative, story-like inner experience.

More than 70% of music listeners report experiencing visual imagery when listening to music. Prior work on undirected mind-wandering during music often involves a dissociation from the body, with imagined landscapes, autobiographical scenes, and film-like narratives — suggesting that music affects imagined position in space and time. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-95260-8)

A key finding across studies is that images (rather than words) are clearly the dominant modality of mental experience during music listening, pointing to a strong link between visual mental imagery and music processing. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-017-14849-0)

Studies on everyday personal music listening found that the predominance of visual mental imagery (compared with inner speech) holds as the modality through which mind-wandering episodes during music occur, regardless of the type of emotion experienced. Importantly, instrumental music was used over music with lyrics to isolate the music's own effects on emotion and imagery. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8656507/)

A useful review: _A Review of Music-Evoked Visual Mental Imagery: Conceptual Issues, Relation to Emotion, and Functional Outcome_ — Taruffi et al. (ResearchGate, 2018).

### 3. The Default Mode Network, Slow Music & Deep Thinking

This is where the neuroscience becomes particularly relevant to your signal analysis work.

Research has found that slow music can enhance the depth of thinking and complex cognitive processing in listeners, and it was also found to enhance mind-wandering activities associated with the Default Mode Network (DMN) — the brain's network active during spontaneous, self-generated thought. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8375772/)

EEG research shows that slow-tempo music supports emotional stability and relaxation via synchronization between the parietal and occipital regions, whereas fast-tempo music enhances emotional and cognitive arousal through increased synchronization between the frontal and parietal regions. [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-92679-1)

A 2025 scoping review in _Creativity Research Journal_ maps the relationship between the DMN and different modes of generative creativity. The DMN — comprising the medial prefrontal cortex, angular gyrus, precuneus, and posterior cingulate cortex — has historically been associated with mind-wandering and introspection, but recent research reveals deep connections to musical creativity and flow states. [Taylor & Francis Online](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10400419.2025.2547196)

### 4. Ambient Music, Creativity, and the Creative Process

The slow-paced and repetitive nature of ambient compositions creates an ideal environment for the brain to relax and explore new ideas. The absence of lyrics or complex melodies allows the listener's mind to wander freely, fostering imagination and open-mindedness. Research has found that moderate volumes of ambient music can promote creativity — the slight increase in processing difficulty appears to encourage more creative cognitive approaches. [Klangspot Recordings](https://klangspot.com/the-therapeutic-benefits-of-ambient-music-a-journey-into-relaxation-and-creativity/)

The intentional incorporation of emotion-evoking music or music as an ambient background correlates with individual creativity, especially in spatial capabilities. Research demonstrates that background music, its type, and the intensity of its emotional appeal all affect creativity in unique ways, fitting into the dual pathway model of mood influencing creativity. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10588669/)

### 5. Acoustic Features that Trigger Extra-Musical Meaning

One reference from the Dahl et al. (2023) paper is particularly useful for audio signal analysis:

Research on acoustic features of instrumental movie soundtracks has shown that they elicit distinct and mostly non-overlapping extra-musical meanings in the mind of the listener — directly relevant to understanding which audio features drive narrative or imaginative responses. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/10298649221124862)

### Summary for Your Project

The convergence point for your work is this: slow-tempo, ambient, instrumental music uniquely activates the DMN and visual imagination systems, and measurable acoustic features (tempo, mode, timbre, absence of lyrics) are linked to how readily listeners enter narrative imaginative states. Margulis's lab has even begun using NLP to analyze the _content_ of imagined stories — which creates a compelling model for combining audio signal analysis with narrative/imaginative outcomes. The cultural-intersubjectivity finding also suggests that certain acoustic signatures may reliably predict _categories_ of imagined narrative, which could be a productive angle for your analysis.
