**Sound** is a wave that transmits energy from one point to another. A **Mechanical Wave** is an oscillation that travels through space and through a **Medium**.

A wave can be visualised in a pressure plot.

![[Pasted image 20260331152050.png]]

A complex sound can be represented as a **Waveform**, which is basically a pressure plot.

![[Pasted image 20260331152205.png]]

These Waveforms are crucial when applying certain transformations on the data, and carries the following data:
- Frequency
- Intensity
- Timbre
- Temporal features (on-sets, duration)

Waveforms can be categorised.

![[Pasted image 20260331154002.png]]

Here, given examples for these categories are:
- **Simple**: A piano tone
- **Complex**: Orchestra
- **Continuous**: Wind
- **Transient**: Click, Pops

*Simple waveforms*

![[Pasted image 20260331154750.png]]

Where:
- **Amplitude**: How high the (de)compression in the air is, measured as the distance of a peak/dip to the normal pressure.
	- Amplitude determines the loudness of the sound.
- **Frequency**: Is expressed in Hz, which represents the number of cycles (re-occurring) peaks/dips per second.
	- Frequency determines how high the sound is.
- **Phase**: The shift / offset of the waveform.

---

*Pitch*
Pitch is the perception of frequency within a sound. Frequency is perceived in a logarithmic way, rather than a linear way.

*Midi notes*
Midi note numbers are mapped to names. For example, "C4" represents the note "C" and the octave "4". An octave is a set of the same 12 notes that repeat themselves.

![[Pasted image 20260331155948.png]]


The main takeaway is that pitch is logarithmic, meaning that the increase in frequency is logarithmically perceived.

![[Pasted image 20260331160442.png]]

*Cents*
Whilst an octave is divided into 12 **Semitones**, it can also be divided in 1200 **cents**. A pitch difference can be perceived when said difference is about 10-25 cents.
